<a id="band-row-to-squat"></a>
## Band row to squat

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: compound

**1. Exercise name:** Band row to squat

**2. Primary muscles:** Mid-back, Lats

**3. Secondary muscles:** Biceps, Rear delts

**4. Equipment:** Resistance band

**5. Difficulty:** beginner

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Jerking the weight
  - Shrugging into the neck
  - Twisting the torso to finish the rep

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Use chest support if your low back gets tired quickly.
  - Lower the load if you cannot pause at the top.

**8. Breathing:** Take a breath and brace before the rep, then exhale as you pass the hardest part of the movement.

**9. Tempo:** Control the lowering phase, stay organized at the hardest point, and finish each rep before starting the next.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Lead with the elbow
  - Pull to the hip
  - Stay square through the torso

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Chest-supported row.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Lead with the elbow".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Single-arm row.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** ankle dorsiflexion and hip flexion to reach depth with a tall torso; shoulder-blade control to pull with the back, not just the arms.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Single-arm row
  - Cable row

**15. Regressions:**
  - Chest-supported row
  - Single-arm row

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Band row
  - Bear crawl
  - Bear crawl drag
  - Chest-supported row

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Knees: keep them tracking over the toes and reduce depth if they ache.
  - Lower back: keep a flat, braced spine and avoid rounding under load.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 6–10 reps (strength: 4–6 · muscle: 8–12)

**19. Ideal rest period:** 90–180 sec (heavier = longer)

**20. Training purpose:** Band row to squat is a mid-back and lats pattern matched to resistance band. used to train mid-back, lats.

**Setup:** Set the feet, brace the trunk, and hold the resistance band where the torso can stay tall.

**Execution:** Sit down between the hips while the knees track over the toes and the whole foot stays planted. Pause at the deepest clean position you can keep without the chest folding or heels lifting. Drive through the floor and stand tall without letting the load pull you forward.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the feet, brace the trunk, and hold the resistance band where the torso can stay tall.
  - Mid: Sit down between the hips while the knees track over the toes and the whole foot stays planted.
  - Peak: Pause at the deepest clean position you can keep without the chest folding or heels lifting.
  - Finish: Drive through the floor and stand tall without letting the load pull you forward.

## Band thruster

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: compound

**1. Exercise name:** Band thruster

**2. Primary muscles:** Shoulders

**3. Secondary muscles:** Triceps, Upper back

**4. Equipment:** Resistance band

**5. Difficulty:** beginner

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Overarching the lower back
  - Pressing out in front
  - Shrugging early

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Skip or reduce load when shoulder symptoms are active.
  - Use a machine or neutral grip if overhead range is limited.

**8. Breathing:** Inhale on the controlled lowering phase and exhale as you drive through the main effort.

**9. Tempo:** Control the lowering phase, stay organized at the hardest point, and finish each rep before starting the next.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Ribs down
  - Punch straight up
  - Finish tall

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Seated dumbbell press.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Ribs down".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Neutral-grip press.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** hamstring and hip-hinge range to keep a flat back; shoulder and thoracic (upper-back) mobility to move overhead safely.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Neutral-grip press
  - Band pull-apart if pressing is not tolerated

**15. Regressions:**
  - Seated dumbbell press
  - Neutral-grip press

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Arnold press
  - Band overhead press
  - Dumbbell shoulder press
  - Dumbbell thruster

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Lower back: keep a flat, braced spine and avoid rounding under load.
  - Shoulders: stay in a pain-free range and stop if you feel pinching.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 6–10 reps (strength: 4–6 · muscle: 8–12)

**19. Ideal rest period:** 90–180 sec (heavier = longer)

**20. Training purpose:** Band thruster is a shoulders pattern matched to resistance band. used to train shoulders.

**Setup:** Plant the feet, set the ribs down, and position the load over the hips before you begin the first rep.

**Execution:** Drive through the heels to lift the hips, squeeze the glutes at the top, and lower under control without arching the low back.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Plant the feet, brace lightly, and set the ribs before you lift.
  - Mid: Push through the heels and lift the hips until the glutes do the work.
  - Peak: Pause at the top without turning the lockout into a low-back arch.
  - Finish: Lower slowly and reset so the hips do not just drop between reps.

## Bear crawl

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: core

**1. Exercise name:** Bear crawl

**2. Primary muscles:** Shoulders, Core, Quadriceps

**3. Secondary muscles:** Glutes, Hip flexors, Serratus anterior

**4. Equipment:** Bodyweight

**5. Difficulty:** Intermediate

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Hips hiking too high
  - Letting the knees touch down every step
  - Taking steps that are too large
  - Twisting the trunk during the crawl

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Use a short lane and quality-first pace.
  - Rest once the hover position breaks rather than dragging through sloppy reps.

**8. Breathing:** Use steady breaths and avoid holding your breath while crawling.

**9. Tempo:** Take short crisp steps with control rather than rushing.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Knees stay low
  - Opposite hand and foot move
  - Hips stay quiet

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Bird dog.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Knees stay low".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Crawl backward.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Crawl backward
  - Increase distance
  - Add shoulder tap pauses

**15. Regressions:**
  - Bird dog
  - Bear hover hold
  - Marching tabletop

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Band row to squat
  - Bear crawl drag
  - Mountain climber
  - Renegade row

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Low joint stress. Stop if any sharp pain appears and reduce range or load.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 10–20 reps or 20–45 sec holds

**19. Ideal rest period:** 30–45 sec

**20. Training purpose:** The bear crawl is a low hovering crawl where opposite hand and foot move together while the trunk stays stable and the knees stay just off the floor.

**Setup:** Start on hands and feet with knees hovering a few inches off the floor. Keep the back flat, ribs down, and hands under the shoulders before moving.

**Execution:** Crawl forward by moving opposite hand and foot together in short controlled steps. Keep the hips level, knees hovering, and torso quiet as you move.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the hover position with knees off the floor, spine long, and trunk braced.
  - Mid: Move one hand and the opposite foot forward together while keeping the hips quiet.
  - Peak: Own the hovering crawl position as the body shifts weight without twisting or sagging.
  - Finish: Place the limbs down softly, continue the pattern, and stop before posture breaks.

## Medicine ball slam

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: power

**1. Exercise name:** Medicine ball slam

**2. Primary muscles:** Core, Lats, Shoulders, Conditioning

**3. Secondary muscles:** Glutes, Hamstrings, Arms

**4. Equipment:** Medicine ball

**5. Difficulty:** Intermediate

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Leaning back too far overhead
  - Trying to catch a high bounce carelessly
  - Slamming with only the arms
  - Rushing the pickup into poor posture

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Use a slam-safe ball and clear floor space.
  - Pick the ball up with control between reps instead of rounding and snatching it up.

**8. Breathing:** Inhale before the overhead reach and exhale sharply through the slam.

**9. Tempo:** Use powerful slams with deliberate resets rather than sloppy nonstop reps.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Reach up with control
  - Slam through the trunk and hips
  - Retrieve safely

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Jumping jack.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Reach up with control".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Use a slightly heavier slam ball.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Use a slightly heavier slam ball
  - Increase work density
  - Add rotational slam variations

**15. Regressions:**
  - Jumping jack
  - Cable crunch
  - Bodyweight squat to reach

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Assault bike sprint
  - Band power step
  - Battle rope waves
  - Bike sprint

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Low joint stress. Stop if any sharp pain appears and reduce range or load.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 3–6 explosive reps

**19. Ideal rest period:** 2–3 min (full recovery)

**20. Training purpose:** The medicine ball slam is an explosive power drill where the ball is lifted overhead and slammed to the floor using the trunk, arms, and hips together.

**Setup:** Stand with feet around hip width and hold the ball securely in both hands. Brace the trunk and make sure the slam area is clear before taking the ball overhead.

**Execution:** Reach the ball overhead, then drive it powerfully down by flexing through the trunk and hips. Follow the ball safely, pick it up with control, and reset before the next rep.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the stance, grip the ball securely, and reach it overhead with a braced trunk.
  - Mid: Drive the ball downward with the trunk, arms, and hips working together.
  - Peak: Finish the slam into the floor with full body tension and no overextension through the low back.
  - Finish: Follow the ball down safely, retrieve it with control, and reset before the next slam.

**2. Primary muscles:** Core, Hip flexors

**3. Secondary muscles:** Shoulders, Chest, Quads

**4. Equipment:** Bodyweight

  - Cutting the knee drive short with sloppy rhythm
  - Holding the breath as speed rises

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Elevate the hands if wrist or shoulder loading is too high.
  - Slow the pace if the plank shape breaks down.
  - Stop if low-back discomfort replaces core tension.

**8. Breathing:** Use short steady breaths and avoid holding your breath as the pace increases.

**9. Tempo:** Move fast enough to raise the heart rate while keeping the plank shape under control.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Keep the plank shape
  - Drive the knee under control
  - Shoulders stay stacked over hands

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: High plank march.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Keep the plank shape".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Increase pace.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Increase pace
  - Add timed intervals
  - Use sliding discs for smoother knee drives

**15. Regressions:**
  - High plank march
  - Incline mountain climber
  - Dead bug

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Band row to squat
  - Bear crawl
  - Bear crawl drag
  - Renegade row

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Low joint stress. Stop if any sharp pain appears and reduce range or load.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 10–20 reps or 20–45 sec holds

**19. Ideal rest period:** 30–45 sec

**20. Training purpose:** The mountain climber is a plank-based conditioning drill where the knees drive forward one at a time. It challenges the core and shoulders while raising the heart rate.

**Setup:** Start in a strong plank with hands under the shoulders, core braced, and body in a straight line from head to heels. Spread the fingers and push the floor away so the shoulders stay active.

**Execution:** Drive one knee toward the chest while the other leg stays extended. Switch legs smoothly without letting the hips bounce high or sag low. Keep the trunk braced and shoulders stable throughout.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set a strong plank with hands under the shoulders, core braced, and hips level.
  - Mid: Drive one knee forward under the torso while the opposite leg stays long and the shoulders stay stacked.
  - Peak: Reach the most compressed knee position you can control without the hips hiking or sagging.
  - Finish: Switch legs smoothly and keep the body in a stable plank as the pattern continues.
