<a id="90-90-hip-flow"></a>
## 90/90 hip flow

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: mobility

**1. Exercise name:** 90/90 hip flow

**2. Primary muscles:** Hip internal rotation, Hip external rotation, Glutes

**3. Secondary muscles:** Adductors, Core, Deep hip rotators

**4. Equipment:** Bodyweight

**5. Difficulty:** Intermediate

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Throwing the knees side to side without control
  - Leaning back so far that the hips stop doing the work
  - Lifting the feet excessively instead of rotating through the hips
  - Collapsing the torso over the front leg

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Use the hands for support and reduce range if the hips feel pinchy.
  - Move slowly through the middle position, where control is usually hardest.

**8. Breathing:** Breathe steadily through the transitions and exhale gently into the end position on each side.

**9. Tempo:** Move slowly through the rotation and pause briefly when you arrive on each side.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Rotate through the hips
  - Sit tall on each side
  - Control the middle

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Seated hip rotation with hands supported.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Rotate through the hips".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Lift the hands briefly during the switch.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** thoracic rotation with a stable lower back.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Lift the hands briefly during the switch
  - Add a forward fold over the front shin on each side

**15. Regressions:**
  - Seated hip rotation with hands supported
  - Supine windshield-wiper hip rotations

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Ankle rocks
  - Cat-cow
  - Child's pose side reach
  - Hamstring stretch

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Low joint stress. Stop if any sharp pain appears and reduce range or load.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 5–10 slow reps or 20–40 sec holds

**19. Ideal rest period:** Minimal — flow between sides

**20. Training purpose:** The 90/90 hip flow is a seated mobility drill that trains the hips to rotate internally and externally under control. It helps build usable hip motion instead of only passive stretching.

**Setup:** Sit on the floor with one leg in front and one leg behind, both bent roughly to 90 degrees. Keep the torso tall, hands on the floor for support if needed, and both sitting bones as even as possible.

**Execution:** Rotate the knees from one side to the other while keeping the feet moving with them. Control the transition through the middle, then settle into the next 90/90 position and sit tall before reversing.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Sit tall in a 90/90 position with one shin in front, one leg behind, and support from the hands if needed.
  - Mid: Rotate both knees toward the middle while keeping the feet organized and the torso under control.
  - Peak: Settle into the opposite 90/90 position and stack tall over the hips without collapsing.
  - Finish: Own the new side for a breath, then rotate back under control for the next rep.

## Ankle rocks

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: mobility · ⚠️ Review: uses expert jargon a child may not know (1 place(s))

**1. Exercise name:** Ankle rocks

**2. Primary muscles:** Ankle dorsiflexion, Soleus, Calf mobility

**3. Secondary muscles:** Foot stabilizers, Tibialis anterior, Achilles tendon control

**4. Equipment:** Bodyweight

**5. Difficulty:** Beginner

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Letting the heel lift to fake more range
  - Rolling the foot inward or outward
  - Bouncing into the end range
  - Letting the knee drift far off line from the toes

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Use a pain-free range and avoid forcing the ankle if the front of the joint feels pinchy.
  - Keep the full foot grounded instead of collapsing the arch.

**8. Breathing:** Breathe steadily and exhale softly as you move into the end range.

**9. Tempo:** Rock forward slowly, pause briefly, and return without bouncing.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Heel stays down
  - Knee tracks over the toes
  - Glide, do not bounce

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Standing calf stretch with bent knee.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Heel stays down".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Add a longer pause at end range.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Add a longer pause at end range
  - Use a loaded split-stance ankle rock with very light support

**15. Regressions:**
  - Standing calf stretch with bent knee
  - Seated ankle dorsiflexion drill

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - 90/90 hip flow
  - Cat-cow
  - Child's pose side reach
  - Hamstring stretch

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Low joint stress. Stop if any sharp pain appears and reduce range or load.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 5–10 slow reps or 20–40 sec holds

**19. Ideal rest period:** Minimal — flow between sides

**20. Training purpose:** Ankle rocks improve ankle dorsiflexion by moving the knee forward over the toes while the heel stays planted. They are useful when limited ankle motion affects squatting, lunging, or gait mechanics.

**Setup:** Set up in a half-kneeling or split-stance position with the working foot flat on the floor. Keep the heel grounded, toes pointed forward, and hands available for balance if needed.

**Execution:** Drive the knee forward over the toes as far as you can without the heel lifting. Pause briefly at the end range, then ease back out and repeat with smooth control.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the working foot flat, heel down, and knee lined up with the toes in a stable split stance.
  - Mid: Glide the knee forward over the toes while keeping the heel anchored to the floor.
  - Peak: Own the deepest controlled dorsiflexion position with pressure through the whole foot.
  - Finish: Rock back slightly, keep the heel down, and repeat the forward glide with control.

## Cat-cow

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: mobility

**1. Exercise name:** Cat-cow

**2. Primary muscles:** Spine mobility, Thoracic spine, Lumbar control

**3. Secondary muscles:** Abdominals, Shoulders, Hips

**4. Equipment:** Bodyweight

**5. Difficulty:** Beginner

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Bending mostly through the neck instead of the full spine
  - Forcing the lower back into extension
  - Rushing through the positions without breathing
  - Keeping the shoulders tense instead of pressing the floor calmly

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Keep the range comfortable and controlled rather than chasing the biggest shape possible.
  - If wrists are sensitive, use fists, handles, or a folded towel under the hands.

**8. Breathing:** Exhale as you round into cat. Inhale as you lengthen and open into cow.

**9. Tempo:** Move slowly from one end range to the other and pause briefly where you feel the clearest spinal motion.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Move one spinal segment at a time
  - Let the breath lead the shape
  - Keep the shoulders calm

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Seated cat-cow.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Move one spinal segment at a time".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Pause at each end range.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Pause at each end range
  - Add segment-by-segment spinal control

**15. Regressions:**
  - Seated cat-cow
  - Standing wall-supported spinal flexion and extension

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - 90/90 hip flow
  - Ankle rocks
  - Child's pose side reach
  - Hamstring stretch

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Elbows/wrists: keep movements smooth and avoid jerking or over-extending.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 5–10 slow reps or 20–40 sec holds

**19. Ideal rest period:** Minimal — flow between sides

**20. Training purpose:** Cat-cow is a controlled spinal mobility drill performed on hands and knees. It teaches you to move segment by segment through spinal flexion and extension while pairing the motion with relaxed breathing.

**Setup:** Start on all fours with hands under shoulders and knees under hips. Spread the fingers, press the floor away lightly, and keep the neck long so the whole spine can move smoothly.

**Execution:** Round the spine upward into flexion as you gently tuck the pelvis and bring the ribs up. Then reverse the motion by tipping the pelvis the other way, letting the chest open, and extending through the upper and mid-back without collapsing into the lower back.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set hands under shoulders, knees under hips, and find a neutral tabletop with a long neck.
  - Mid: Exhale and round the spine upward, tucking the pelvis and drawing the ribs toward the ceiling.
  - Peak: Move into the opposite direction by inhaling, opening the chest, and extending through the thoracic spine with control.
  - Finish: Return smoothly through neutral and continue alternating without rushing or dumping into the low back.

## Child's pose side reach

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: general

**1. Exercise name:** Child's pose side reach

**2. Primary muscles:** Lats, Thoracic side body, Shoulder mobility

**3. Secondary muscles:** Obliques, Upper back, Hips

**4. Equipment:** Bodyweight

**5. Difficulty:** Beginner

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Letting the hips drift away from the heels
  - Collapsing the shoulders instead of reaching long
  - Turning the position into a twist instead of a side reach
  - Holding the breath instead of expanding the side ribs

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Pad the knees or place support under the hips if child's pose is uncomfortable.
  - Keep the reach gentle if the shoulders feel irritated.

**8. Breathing:** Take slow breaths into the side ribs and upper back while holding the reach.

**9. Tempo:** Move the hands gradually, pause in the stretch, and return to center smoothly.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Hips stay heavy
  - Reach through the outside arm
  - Breathe into the open ribs

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Seated side reach.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Hips stay heavy".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Hold longer with deeper breaths.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Hold longer with deeper breaths
  - Reach the top hand farther over the bottom hand

**15. Regressions:**
  - Seated side reach
  - Wall-supported lat stretch

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - 90/90 hip flow
  - Ankle rocks
  - Cat-cow
  - Hamstring stretch

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Low joint stress. Stop if any sharp pain appears and reduce range or load.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 8–12 reps

**19. Ideal rest period:** 60–90 sec

**20. Training purpose:** Child's pose side reach combines a supported child's pose with a lateral hand walk to open the lats, side body, and upper back. It is useful when overhead work or pulling volume has left the upper body feeling stiff.

**Setup:** Start in child's pose with knees comfortable, hips back toward the heels, and hands reaching forward on the floor. Relax the neck and let the torso settle before shifting the hands.

**Execution:** Walk both hands to one side while keeping the hips heavy. Reach through the outside arm and breathe into the open side of the ribcage, then return through center before switching sides.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Settle into child's pose with hips back, arms reaching forward, and the spine relaxed but long.
  - Mid: Walk both hands to one side while keeping the hips anchored and the chest low.
  - Peak: Reach long through the outside arm and breathe into the side body and lat stretch.
  - Finish: Walk back to center with control, reset, and repeat to the other side.

## Hamstring stretch

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: mobility

**1. Exercise name:** Hamstring stretch

**2. Primary muscles:** Hamstrings

**3. Secondary muscles:** Calves, Glutes, Low back stabilizers

**4. Equipment:** Bodyweight

**5. Difficulty:** Beginner

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Rounding the spine to chase more range
  - Locking the knee aggressively
  - Pulling so hard that the stretch becomes painful
  - Holding breath while folding deeper

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Keep the stretch comfortable and do not force end range.
  - Bend the knee slightly if the hamstring or back feels too strained.

**8. Breathing:** Breathe slowly and use each exhale to soften into the position without forcing range.

**9. Tempo:** Move in gradually, hold the stretch steadily, and back out with control.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Hinge from the hips
  - Keep the spine long
  - Stretch, do not yank

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Supine hamstring stretch.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Hinge from the hips".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Increase the hold time.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** hamstring and hip-hinge range to keep a flat back.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Increase the hold time
  - Move into a longer hip hinge with the spine still organized

**15. Regressions:**
  - Supine hamstring stretch
  - Seated hamstring stretch with bent knee

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - 90/90 hip flow
  - Ankle rocks
  - Cat-cow
  - Child's pose side reach

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Lower back: keep a flat, braced spine and avoid rounding under load.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 5–10 slow reps or 20–40 sec holds

**19. Ideal rest period:** Minimal — flow between sides

**20. Training purpose:** The hamstring stretch lengthens the back of the thigh and is most effective when you hinge from the hips rather than simply rounding the spine to reach farther.

**Setup:** Set up in the version you are using with one or both legs extended. Keep the spine long, chest open, and toes relaxed rather than aggressively pulled back unless the variation calls for it.

**Execution:** Hinge forward from the hips until you feel a stretch through the hamstrings. Maintain a long spine and stop before the stretch turns into low-back strain or pulling behind the knee.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the legs and posture for the chosen variation with the spine long and hips ready to hinge.
  - Mid: Fold forward from the hips while keeping the chest reaching long instead of rounding heavily.
  - Peak: Hold the strongest stretch you can manage through the hamstrings without pain or pulling behind the knee.
  - Finish: Come out of the stretch slowly and reset posture before the next rep or side.

**8. Breathing:** Take slow breaths and exhale as you settle slightly deeper into the stretch.

**9. Tempo:** Ease into the stretch gradually and hold or pulse with control rather than bouncing.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Tuck the pelvis
  - Tall torso

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Reach the same-side arm overhead.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Reach the same-side arm overhead
  - Add a gentle side bend away from the down knee

**15. Regressions:**
  - Standing split-stance hip flexor stretch
  - Couch stretch with very short range

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - 90/90 hip flow
  - Ankle rocks
  - Cat-cow
  - Child's pose side reach

  - Elbows/wrists: keep movements smooth and avoid jerking or over-extending.

**Setup:** Set up in a half-kneeling position with one knee down and the other foot planted in front. Keep the torso tall, lightly tuck the pelvis, and brace enough to avoid arching the lower back.

**Execution:** Shift the body gently forward while maintaining the slight posterior pelvic tilt. You should feel the stretch in the front of the down-leg hip rather than the lower back. Hold or pulse lightly without losing posture.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set a half-kneeling stance with the front foot planted, torso tall, and pelvis slightly tucked.
  - Mid: Shift forward gently while keeping the ribs stacked and the glute on the down-leg side lightly active.
  - Peak: Settle into the stretch at the front of the hip without arching the lower back.
  - Finish: Back out of the stretch smoothly, reset posture, and repeat before switching sides.

**2. Primary muscles:** Shoulder range of motion, Scapular control

**5. Difficulty:** Beginner

**6. Common mistakes:**

  - Shrugging aggressively instead of controlling the shoulder blade

  - Pushing into pinchy shoulder range

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Stay in a pain-free range and reduce the arc if the shoulder pinches.
  - Prioritize smooth control over reaching higher or farther.

**8. Breathing:** Use smooth breaths and avoid bracing so hard that the ribcage cannot stay stacked.

**9. Tempo:** Move slowly enough to feel shoulder control at every part of the range.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Ribs stay stacked
  - Shoulder blade moves with the arm
  - No forced range

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Wall-assisted shoulder reach.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Ribs stay stacked".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Add a light band.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Add a light band
  - Pause at end range without losing rib position

**15. Regressions:**
  - Wall-assisted shoulder reach
  - Supine shoulder flexion

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - 90/90 hip flow
  - Ankle rocks
  - Cat-cow
  - Child's pose side reach

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Low joint stress. Stop if any sharp pain appears and reduce range or load.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 5–10 slow reps or 20–40 sec holds

**19. Ideal rest period:** Minimal — flow between sides

**20. Training purpose:** Shoulder mobility drills train the shoulders and shoulder blades to move through a controlled range without compensating through the ribs or lower back. The focus is smooth quality, not forcing more range than you own.

**Setup:** Stand or kneel tall with the ribs stacked over the pelvis. Set the shoulders down and around the ribcage, and keep the neck relaxed before starting the arm motion.

**Execution:** Move the arms through the planned pattern with control, keeping the ribs from flaring and the shoulders from shrugging excessively. Let the shoulder blades rotate naturally while the torso stays organized.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set a tall posture with ribs stacked, shoulders relaxed, and arms ready to move.
  - Mid: Begin the shoulder motion slowly, keeping the ribcage quiet and the neck relaxed.
  - Peak: Reach the usable end range with the shoulder blades moving well and no aggressive shrugging.
  - Finish: Return through the same path with control and reset before the next rep.

  - Shorten the lunge or place the back knee down if balance or hip tension makes the drill too aggressive.
  - Rotate only as far as you can keep the front foot and pelvis under control.

**8. Breathing:** Exhale as you sink and reach toward the floor. Inhale as you rotate the chest open.

**9. Tempo:** Move deliberately between the floor reach and the open rotation, pausing briefly in each end position.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Long lunge first
  - Rotate from the upper back
  - Keep the front foot rooted

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Half-kneeling hip flexor stretch.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Long lunge first".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Add a hamstring rock-back between reps.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** ankle dorsiflexion and hip flexion to reach depth with a tall torso; thoracic rotation with a stable lower back.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Add a hamstring rock-back between reps
  - Pause longer in the thoracic rotation

**15. Regressions:**
  - Half-kneeling hip flexor stretch
  - Quadruped thoracic rotation

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - 90/90 hip flow
  - Ankle rocks
  - Cat-cow
  - Child's pose side reach

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Knees: keep them tracking over the toes and reduce depth if they ache.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 5–10 slow reps or 20–40 sec holds

**19. Ideal rest period:** Minimal — flow between sides

**20. Training purpose:** World's greatest stretch is a full-body mobility drill that combines a long lunge, hamstring lengthening, and thoracic rotation. It is useful when you want one movement that opens the hips and upper back together.

**Setup:** Step into a long lunge with the front foot outside the same-side hand. Keep the back leg long, back heel lifted, and both hands near the front foot so you can move between the floor and the rotation.

**Execution:** Sink into the lunge to open the back hip, then bring the forearm or hand toward the floor inside the front foot. From there, rotate the chest open and reach the same-side arm upward while keeping the front foot rooted and the spine long.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set a long lunge with the front foot planted and both hands near the inside of that foot.
  - Mid: Lower the torso toward the floor or front shin while keeping the back leg active and the front heel grounded.
  - Peak: Rotate the chest open and reach the same-side arm upward, creating space through the thoracic spine.
  - Finish: Bring the hand back down with control, reset the lunge, and repeat before switching sides.
