## Band chest press

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: compound

**1. Exercise name:** Band chest press

**2. Primary muscles:** Chest, Triceps, Anterior deltoids

**3. Secondary muscles:** Core, Serratus anterior

**4. Equipment:** Resistance band

**5. Difficulty:** Beginner

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Letting the band pull the arms back too fast
  - Arching the lower back
  - Pressing too high toward the neck
  - Standing with unstable feet
  - Losing band tension

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Make sure the band anchor is secure before pressing.
  - Inspect the band for damage.
  - Stand far enough forward for tension but not so far that control is lost.

**8. Breathing:** Exhale as you press forward. Inhale as you return.

**9. Tempo:** Press for 1–2 seconds, return slowly for 2–3 seconds.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Keep the ribs down
  - Press straight forward
  - Control the band on the return

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Wall push-up.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Keep the ribs down".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Use a stronger band.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** shoulder and thoracic (upper-back) mobility to move overhead safely.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Use a stronger band
  - Use a staggered stance
  - Slow the eccentric return

**15. Regressions:**
  - Wall push-up
  - Incline push-up

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Barbell bench press
  - Deficit push-up
  - Dumbbell floor press
  - Flat dumbbell bench press

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Shoulders: stay in a pain-free range and stop if you feel pinching.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 6–10 reps (strength: 4–6 · muscle: 8–12)

**19. Ideal rest period:** 90–180 sec (heavier = longer)

**20. Training purpose:** The band chest press is a standing or anchored pressing exercise using a resistance band. It trains chest and triceps while requiring core control against band tension.

**Setup:** Anchor the band behind you at chest height or wrap it securely around your upper back. Hold one end in each hand, step forward to create tension, and set hands near chest level.

**Execution:** Press the hands forward until arms extend without locking aggressively. Keep ribs down, shoulders controlled, and band path level. Return slowly until hands come back near the chest.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Stand tall with the band anchored behind you, hands at chest height, elbows bent, and core braced.
  - Mid: Press forward against band tension while keeping shoulders down and ribs controlled.
  - Peak: Reach the extended position with chest and triceps engaged, avoiding excessive shoulder shrug.
  - Finish: Return slowly until hands come back near the chest while keeping tension on the band.

## Barbell bench press

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: compound

**1. Exercise name:** Barbell bench press

**2. Primary muscles:** Chest

**3. Secondary muscles:** Triceps, Front delts

**4. Equipment:** Barbell

**5. Difficulty:** intermediate

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Bouncing at the bottom
  - Arching hard through the low back
  - Letting wrists fold back

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Use the floor press variation if shoulder range feels limited.
  - Keep load conservative if shoulder irritation is active.

**8. Breathing:** Inhale on the controlled lowering phase and exhale as you drive through the main effort.

**9. Tempo:** Control the lowering phase, stay organized at the hardest point, and finish each rep before starting the next.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Stack wrist over elbow
  - Own the bottom
  - Press without shrugging

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Floor press.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Stack wrist over elbow".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Single-arm dumbbell press.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** shoulder and thoracic (upper-back) mobility to move overhead safely.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Single-arm dumbbell press
  - Neutral-grip press

**15. Regressions:**
  - Floor press
  - Single-arm dumbbell press

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Band chest press
  - Dumbbell floor press
  - Flat dumbbell bench press
  - Incline dumbbell press

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Shoulders: stay in a pain-free range and stop if you feel pinching.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 6–10 reps (strength: 4–6 · muscle: 8–12)

**19. Ideal rest period:** 90–180 sec (heavier = longer)

**20. Training purpose:** Barbell bench press is a chest pattern matched to barbell. used to train chest.

**Setup:** Set the shoulders and wrists so the press starts from a stacked, controlled position.

**Execution:** Lower under control until the chest, elbows, or upper arm reach the intended bottom range. Press smoothly while keeping the ribcage and shoulders organized. Lock out only as far as you can stay tall and ready for the next rep.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the shoulders and wrists so the press starts from a stacked, controlled position.
  - Mid: Lower under control until the chest, elbows, or upper arm reach the intended bottom range.
  - Peak: Press smoothly while keeping the ribcage and shoulders organized.
  - Finish: Lock out only as far as you can stay tall and ready for the next rep.

## Cable chest fly

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: isolation

**1. Exercise name:** Cable chest fly

**2. Primary muscles:** Chest

**3. Secondary muscles:** Anterior deltoids, Serratus anterior

**4. Equipment:** Cable machine

**5. Difficulty:** Intermediate

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Straightening the elbows and turning it into a press
  - Letting the shoulders roll forward
  - Using too much weight and cutting the arc short
  - Losing control in the stretched position

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Stop the stretch before the front of the shoulder feels pinchy.
  - Use a load that lets you own the return without the cables pulling you open.

**8. Breathing:** Exhale as the hands come together. Inhale as the arms open back out.

**9. Tempo:** Move together for 1-2 seconds and return for 2-3 seconds with a controlled stretch.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Hug the arms together
  - Soft elbows stay fixed
  - Control the stretch

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Band chest fly.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Hug the arms together".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Use a lower cable angle.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Use a lower cable angle
  - Add a pause at peak contraction
  - Slow the eccentric return

**15. Regressions:**
  - Band chest fly
  - Machine chest press
  - Push-up

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Decline push-up
  - Dumbbell chest fly
  - Dumbbell squeeze press
  - Flat dumbbell bench press

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Shoulders: stay in a pain-free range and stop if you feel pinching.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 10–15 reps

**19. Ideal rest period:** 45–60 sec

**20. Training purpose:** The cable chest fly isolates the chest by bringing the arms together through a wide arc while cable tension stays on the pecs. It works best when the elbows stay softly bent and the torso remains quiet.

**Setup:** Set the cable handles around chest height or slightly above. Stand in a staggered stance, take one handle in each hand, step forward into tension, and keep the chest tall with a soft bend in the elbows.

**Execution:** Bring the hands together in a hugging arc in front of the chest without turning the movement into a press. Control the stretch as the arms open back out and stop before the shoulders lose position.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the cables, step into a stable stance, and begin with the arms open and elbows softly bent.
  - Mid: Sweep the arms inward in a wide arc while keeping the chest lifted and shoulders controlled.
  - Peak: Bring the hands together in front of the chest and squeeze the pecs without slamming the handles together.
  - Finish: Open the arms back out slowly until you feel a stretch across the chest, then repeat with the same arc.

## Decline push-up

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: compound

**1. Exercise name:** Decline push-up

**2. Primary muscles:** Upper chest, Anterior deltoids, Triceps

**3. Secondary muscles:** Core, Serratus anterior

**4. Equipment:** Bodyweight, Bench or sturdy elevation

**5. Difficulty:** Intermediate

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Letting the low back sag because the feet are too high
  - Dropping the head between the arms
  - Flaring the elbows too wide
  - Using an unstable foot support

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Choose a foot height you can control without losing trunk position.
  - Stop if shoulder or wrist discomfort builds as the load increases.

**8. Breathing:** Inhale as you lower. Exhale as you press back up.

**9. Tempo:** Lower for 2-3 seconds and press up with smooth control.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Feet elevated, trunk rigid
  - Lower with control
  - Press back to a clean plank

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Standard push-up.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Feet elevated, trunk rigid".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Add a pause at the bottom.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** shoulder and thoracic (upper-back) mobility to move overhead safely.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Add a pause at the bottom
  - Use a deficit hand position
  - Progress toward ring or weighted push-ups

**15. Regressions:**
  - Standard push-up
  - Incline push-up
  - Hands-elevated push-up

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Cable chest fly
  - Dumbbell chest fly
  - Dumbbell squeeze press
  - Flat dumbbell bench press

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Shoulders: stay in a pain-free range and stop if you feel pinching.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 6–10 reps (strength: 4–6 · muscle: 8–12)

**19. Ideal rest period:** 90–180 sec (heavier = longer)

**20. Training purpose:** The decline push-up is a push-up variation performed with the feet elevated. Elevation shifts more load toward the upper chest and shoulders while increasing the total challenge of the push-up.

**Setup:** Place the feet on a stable bench or box and the hands on the floor slightly wider than shoulder width. Brace the trunk and set a straight line from head through heels before starting the rep.

**Execution:** Lower the chest toward the floor under control while keeping the hips level and elbows at a comfortable angle. Press back up to the top plank without letting the low back sag or the shoulders shrug.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the feet on the elevation, hands on the floor, and create a rigid plank line before the rep begins.
  - Mid: Lower the chest toward the floor while the elbows track below shoulder height and the hips stay level.
  - Peak: Reach the lowest controlled position without collapsing through the shoulders or low back.
  - Finish: Press the floor away and return to a strong elevated plank at the top.

## Deficit push-up

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: compound

**1. Exercise name:** Deficit push-up

**2. Primary muscles:** Chest, Triceps, Anterior deltoids

**3. Secondary muscles:** Core, Serratus anterior

**4. Equipment:** Bodyweight

**5. Difficulty:** Intermediate

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Dropping too deeply without control
  - Letting the shoulders roll forward at the bottom
  - Flaring elbows too wide
  - Sagging through the hips
  - Using unstable hand supports

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Only use a deficit depth you can control without shoulder pinching.
  - Make sure the hand supports are secure and even.
  - Stop before form or shoulder position breaks down.

**8. Breathing:** Inhale as you lower. Exhale as you press up.

**9. Tempo:** Lower under control for 2 seconds, press smoothly.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Own the deeper bottom
  - Keep the shoulders packed
  - Move as one rigid unit

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Standard push-up.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Own the deeper bottom".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Pause in the bottom.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** shoulder and thoracic (upper-back) mobility to move overhead safely.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Pause in the bottom
  - Add tempo
  - Add load with a weighted vest

**15. Regressions:**
  - Standard push-up
  - Incline push-up

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Band chest press
  - Close-grip push-up
  - Dumbbell floor press
  - Push-up

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Shoulders: stay in a pain-free range and stop if you feel pinching.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 6–10 reps (strength: 4–6 · muscle: 8–12)

**19. Ideal rest period:** 90–180 sec (heavier = longer)

**20. Training purpose:** The deficit push-up is a push-up variation performed with the hands elevated on stable handles or blocks so the chest can travel through a deeper range of motion.

**Setup:** Place your hands on stable handles, plates, or dumbbells that create a deficit. Brace the core, squeeze the glutes lightly, and keep the body in a straight line from head to heels.

**Execution:** Lower the chest between the hands by bending the elbows under control. Keep elbows angled roughly 30–60 degrees from the torso, then press back up without losing trunk tension.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the hands on the deficit, brace the trunk, and create a straight line from head to heels.
  - Mid: Lower the chest between the hands with control while elbows track at a comfortable angle.
  - Peak: Reach the deepest controlled position you can hold without sagging the hips or dumping into the shoulders.
  - Finish: Press the floor away and return to a strong plank without losing body tension.

## Dumbbell chest fly

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: isolation

**1. Exercise name:** Dumbbell chest fly

**2. Primary muscles:** Chest

**3. Secondary muscles:** Anterior deltoids, Biceps long head stabilizers

**4. Equipment:** Dumbbells, Flat bench

**5. Difficulty:** Intermediate

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Using too much weight and bending into a press
  - Dropping too deep into the stretch
  - Letting the elbows straighten too much
  - Losing shoulder position at the bottom

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Use lighter loads than you would for pressing.
  - Stop the descent before the front of the shoulder feels strained.

**8. Breathing:** Inhale as the dumbbells lower. Exhale as you bring them back together.

**9. Tempo:** Lower for 2-3 seconds, pause briefly in the stretch if controlled, and return smoothly.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Wide arc, soft elbows
  - Stretch the chest, not the shoulder
  - Fly back together smoothly

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Cable chest fly.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Wide arc, soft elbows".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Add a pause in the stretched position.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Add a pause in the stretched position
  - Use slightly heavier dumbbells
  - Progress to incline dumbbell fly if tolerated

**15. Regressions:**
  - Cable chest fly
  - Machine chest press
  - Push-up

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Cable chest fly
  - Decline push-up
  - Dumbbell squeeze press
  - Flat dumbbell bench press

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Shoulders: stay in a pain-free range and stop if you feel pinching.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 10–15 reps

**19. Ideal rest period:** 45–60 sec

**20. Training purpose:** The dumbbell chest fly is a chest isolation exercise performed lying on a bench with a wide arm arc. It challenges the pecs through a loaded stretch, so control matters more than heavy weight.

**Setup:** Lie on a flat bench with dumbbells above the chest and a slight bend in the elbows. Set the shoulder blades gently back and down, plant the feet, and keep the ribs controlled.

**Execution:** Lower the dumbbells out wide in an arc while keeping the elbow angle mostly fixed. When you reach a comfortable chest stretch, squeeze the arms back together over the chest without turning the rep into a pressing motion.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set on the bench with dumbbells above the chest, feet planted, and elbows softly bent.
  - Mid: Lower the arms outward in a wide arc while keeping the chest open and shoulder blades organized.
  - Peak: Reach the deepest stretch you can control across the chest without shoulder discomfort or elbow collapse.
  - Finish: Squeeze the arms back together over the chest, maintaining the same elbow bend and avoiding a pressing path.

## Dumbbell floor press

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: compound

**1. Exercise name:** Dumbbell floor press

**2. Primary muscles:** Chest, Triceps, Anterior deltoids

**3. Secondary muscles:** Upper back stabilizers, Core

**4. Equipment:** Dumbbells

**5. Difficulty:** Beginner to intermediate

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Slamming elbows into the floor
  - Flaring elbows too wide
  - Letting wrists bend backward

  - Losing control at the bottom

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Lower softly; the floor is a range limiter, not a rebound point.
  - Use manageable weight so you can control the bottom.
  - Keep elbows at a comfortable angle.

**8. Breathing:** Inhale as you lower. Exhale as you press up.

**9. Tempo:** Lower for 2 seconds, pause softly on the floor, press smoothly.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Touch the floor softly
  - Keep wrists stacked
  - Press without rib flare

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Push-up from elevated surface.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Touch the floor softly".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add load slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Heavier dumbbells.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering phase or add a pause to increase difficulty without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, chase progressive overload (small load or rep increases week to week).

**13. Mobility requirements:** shoulder and thoracic (upper-back) mobility to move overhead safely.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Heavier dumbbells
  - Paused reps
  - Bench dumbbell press

**15. Regressions:**
  - Push-up from elevated surface
  - Light dumbbell press

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Band chest press
  - Barbell bench press
  - Deficit push-up
  - Flat dumbbell bench press

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Shoulders: stay in a pain-free range and stop if you feel pinching.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 6–10 reps (strength: 4–6 · muscle: 8–12)

**19. Ideal rest period:** 90–180 sec (heavier = longer)

**20. Training purpose:** The dumbbell floor press is a chest press performed lying on the floor. The floor limits shoulder extension, making it a useful shoulder-friendly pressing option.

**Setup:** Lie on your back with knees bent and feet flat. Hold dumbbells above the chest with wrists stacked over elbows. Set upper arms at a slight angle from the torso, not straight out at shoulder height.

**Execution:** Lower the dumbbells until the upper arms or triceps softly contact the floor. Pause briefly, then press back up while keeping wrists stacked and ribs controlled.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Lie on the floor with dumbbells above the chest, knees bent, feet planted, and wrists stacked.
  - Mid: Lower the dumbbells under control while elbows track slightly below shoulder height.
  - Peak: Let the upper arms lightly touch the floor without relaxing tension or bouncing.
  - Finish: Press the dumbbells back up over the chest while keeping ribs down and shoulders controlled.
