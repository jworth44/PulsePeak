## Ab wheel rollout

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: core

**1. Exercise name:** Ab wheel rollout

**2. Primary muscles:** Abdominals, Deep core stabilizers

**3. Secondary muscles:** Lats, Serratus anterior, Shoulders

**4. Equipment:** Ab wheel

**5. Difficulty:** Intermediate to advanced

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Going too far and arching the low back
  - Dropping the head and shoulders
  - Using momentum to return
  - Losing rib position early in the rollout

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Use a shorter rollout range than you think you need at first.
  - Stop immediately if the movement shifts into low-back strain.

**8. Breathing:** Inhale as you roll out. Exhale as you pull back in.

**9. Tempo:** Roll out slowly for 2-3 seconds and return with control.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Ribs and pelvis stay connected
  - Reach without arching
  - Pull back under control

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Dead bug.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Ribs and pelvis stay connected".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add weight slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Increase rollout range.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering part or add a pause to make it harder without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, add small amounts of weight or a rep or two week to week.

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Increase rollout range
  - Add pauses at full extension
  - Progress toward standing rollout work

**15. Regressions:**
  - Dead bug
  - Plank
  - Body saw plank

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Bicycle crunch
  - Bird dog
  - Cable crunch
  - Crunch

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Elbows/wrists: move smoothly and don't jerk or fully snap the joint straight.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 10–20 reps or 20–45 sec holds

**19. Ideal rest period:** 30–45 sec

**20. Training purpose:** The ab wheel rollout challenges the core to resist spinal extension as the body reaches forward. It works best when the range stops before the low back starts to take over.

**Setup:** Kneel with the ab wheel under the shoulders and hands firmly on the handles. Brace the trunk, tuck the ribs slightly, and keep the glutes lightly engaged before rolling out.

**Execution:** Roll the wheel forward while maintaining a straight trunk and resisting low-back arching. Pull back in using the abs and lats until you return to the starting position.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the knees, wheel, and trunk brace so the ribs and pelvis stay connected before you move.
  - Mid: Roll the wheel forward while keeping the torso long and resisting low-back extension.
  - Peak: Reach the furthest position you can control without the low back arching or the shoulders collapsing.
  - Finish: Pull the wheel back underneath you using the abs and lats while keeping the same trunk shape.

## Bicycle crunch

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: core

**1. Exercise name:** Bicycle crunch

**2. Primary muscles:** Abdominals, Obliques

**3. Secondary muscles:** Hip flexors

**4. Equipment:** Bodyweight

**5. Difficulty:** Beginner to intermediate

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Pulling the head with the hands
  - Rushing the alternating motion
  - Only moving the elbows instead of rotating the trunk
  - Letting the low back arch as the leg extends

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Move slowly enough to keep the trunk and neck under control.
  - Use a smaller leg extension if the low back starts to arch.

**8. Breathing:** Exhale on each twist toward the knee. Inhale as you transition sides under control.

**9. Tempo:** Move with a steady alternating rhythm that keeps each rep controlled.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Rotate from the ribs
  - Hands stay light
  - Extend only as far as you control

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Crunch.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Rotate from the ribs".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add weight slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Slow the tempo.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering part or add a pause to make it harder without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, add small amounts of weight or a rep or two week to week.

**13. Mobility requirements:** the ability to turn at the upper body while keeping your lower back still.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Slow the tempo
  - Pause on each side
  - Increase total reps

**15. Regressions:**
  - Crunch
  - Reverse crunch
  - Dead bug

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Ab wheel rollout
  - Bird dog
  - Cable crunch
  - Crunch

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Low joint stress. Stop if any sharp pain appears and make the movement smaller or easier.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 10–20 reps or 20–45 sec holds

**19. Ideal rest period:** 30–45 sec

**20. Training purpose:** The bicycle crunch combines a crunch with alternating trunk rotation and leg motion to train the abdominals and obliques together.

**Setup:** Lie on your back with hands lightly by the head, knees bent, and shoulder blades ready to lift from the floor. Keep the neck long and the low back controlled.

**Execution:** Lift the shoulders, rotate one elbow toward the opposite knee, and extend the other leg. Alternate sides smoothly while keeping the movement driven by the trunk rather than yanking the head around.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the hands lightly by the head, lift the shoulder blades, and bring one knee in.
  - Mid: Rotate the trunk so one elbow moves toward the opposite knee while the other leg extends.
  - Peak: Reach the strongest oblique contraction without pulling on the neck or losing trunk control.
  - Finish: Switch sides smoothly and repeat with the same controlled rotation.

## Bird dog

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: core

**1. Exercise name:** Bird dog

**2. Primary muscles:** Deep core stabilizers, Spinal stabilizers

**3. Secondary muscles:** Glutes, Shoulders

**4. Equipment:** Bodyweight

**5. Difficulty:** Beginner

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Rotating the hips open
  - Lifting the leg too high and arching the back
  - Rushing the switch
  - Shrugging the reaching shoulder

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Keep the reach long rather than high.
  - Use a smaller reach if you cannot keep the hips and ribs square.

**8. Breathing:** Exhale as you reach long. Inhale as you return to the tabletop position.

**9. Tempo:** Move slowly and pause briefly in the extended position before returning.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Reach long, not high
  - Hips stay level
  - Return under control

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Quadruped brace hold.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Reach long, not high".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add weight slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Pause longer in the reach.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering part or add a pause to make it harder without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, add small amounts of weight or a rep or two week to week.

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Pause longer in the reach
  - Draw elbow to knee between reps
  - Use resisted bird dogs

**15. Regressions:**
  - Quadruped brace hold
  - Dead bug
  - Marching tabletop hold

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Ab wheel rollout
  - Bicycle crunch
  - Cable crunch
  - Crunch

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Low joint stress. Stop if any sharp pain appears and make the movement smaller or easier.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 10–20 reps or 20–45 sec holds

**19. Ideal rest period:** 30–45 sec

**20. Training purpose:** The bird dog teaches the trunk to stay stable while the opposite arm and leg reach away. It is a low-load but high-value drill for spinal control and hip-shoulder coordination.

**Setup:** Start on all fours with hands under shoulders and knees under hips. Brace lightly through the trunk and keep the neck long before reaching.

**Execution:** Reach one arm forward and the opposite leg back while keeping the hips and ribs square to the floor. Return under control and switch sides without shifting excessively.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set a stable tabletop with a long spine and quiet hips before reaching.
  - Mid: Reach the opposite arm and leg away while the torso stays still and the pelvis remains level.
  - Peak: Hold the long reach with the glute, shoulder, and core all engaged at once.
  - Finish: Return to the start smoothly without dropping or twisting, then switch sides.

## Cable crunch

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: core

**1. Exercise name:** Cable crunch

**2. Primary muscles:** Abdominals

**3. Secondary muscles:** Deep core stabilizers, Hip flexors

**4. Equipment:** Cable machine with rope

**5. Difficulty:** Intermediate

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Folding mostly at the hips
  - Yanking the rope with the arms
  - Using too much load to feel the abs
  - Bouncing through the range

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Use a load that lets you feel trunk flexion without neck strain.
  - Keep the pelvis quiet so the stress stays in the abs.

**8. Breathing:** Exhale as you crunch down. Inhale as you return upward.

**9. Tempo:** Crunch for 1-2 seconds and return for 2-3 seconds under control.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Ribs curl down
  - Hips stay quiet
  - Return under tension

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Crunch.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Ribs curl down".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add weight slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Increase load gradually.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering part or add a pause to make it harder without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, add small amounts of weight or a rep or two week to week.

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Increase load gradually
  - Add a pause at the bottom
  - Use slow eccentrics

**15. Regressions:**
  - Crunch
  - Reverse crunch
  - Dead bug

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Ab wheel rollout
  - Bicycle crunch
  - Bird dog
  - Crunch

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Low joint stress. Stop if any sharp pain appears and make the movement smaller or easier.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 10–20 reps or 20–45 sec holds

**19. Ideal rest period:** 30–45 sec

**20. Training purpose:** The cable crunch trains the abs through loaded trunk flexion while kneeling under a high cable. It should feel like the ribs are curling down, not like a hip hinge.

**Setup:** Kneel facing away from a high cable with a rope attachment held near the head or collarbone. Set the hips still, ribs stacked, and glutes lightly engaged before crunching.

**Execution:** Curl the ribs toward the pelvis by flexing through the trunk. Keep the hips relatively quiet and return slowly until the abs lengthen again under tension.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the kneeling stance, hold the rope close, and organize the ribs over the pelvis.
  - Mid: Curl down by drawing the ribs toward the hips while the pelvis stays mostly still.
  - Peak: Reach the strongest abdominal contraction without turning the movement into a hip hinge.
  - Finish: Return upward slowly until the trunk lengthens under cable tension again.

**14. Progressions:**

  - Use cable crunches

**15. Regressions:**
  - Abdominal brace hold
  - Dead bug
  - Short-range crunch

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Ab wheel rollout
  - Bicycle crunch

**2. Primary muscles:** Deep core, Rectus abdominis

**3. Secondary muscles:** Obliques, Hip flexors

**8. Breathing:** Exhale as the arm and leg reach away. Inhale as you return to the start.

**9. Tempo:** Move each rep slowly with a full controlled reach and return.

**10. Coaching cues:**

  - Ribs stay down
  - Reach only as far as you can control

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Bent-knee march.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Low back stays heavy".

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Pause longer at full reach.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering part or add a pause to make it harder without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, add small amounts of weight or a rep or two week to week.

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Pause longer at full reach
  - Use a slower tempo
  - Hold a light band or weight overhead

**15. Regressions:**
  - Bent-knee march
  - Supine heel tap
  - Pallof hold

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Farmer carry
  - Pallof press
  - Plank shoulder tap
  - Rower sprint

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Elbows/wrists: move smoothly and don't jerk or fully snap the joint straight.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 10–20 reps or 20–45 sec holds

**19. Ideal rest period:** 30–45 sec

**20. Training purpose:** The dead bug is a supine core exercise that teaches the trunk to resist extension while the arms and legs move. It builds control through the core without loading the spine heavily.

**Setup:** Lie on your back with arms reaching up and hips and knees bent to tabletop. Flatten the low back gently toward the floor by stacking the ribs over the pelvis, then brace lightly.

**Execution:** Reach one arm and the opposite leg away from the body while keeping the low back from lifting. Move slowly, return to the start, and alternate sides without losing trunk position.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set arms overhead of the shoulders, knees over the hips, and flatten the low back gently into the floor.
  - Mid: Reach one arm and the opposite leg away while keeping the ribs down and the trunk quiet.
  - Peak: Pause at the farthest controlled reach without the low back lifting away from the floor.
  - Finish: Return to tabletop under control and reset before switching sides.

## Farmer carry

> 📝 Text guide (image pending) · Class: carry

**1. Exercise name:** Farmer carry

**2. Primary muscles:** Grip, Traps, Core

**3. Secondary muscles:** Forearms, Shoulders, Glutes

**4. Equipment:** Dumbbells, kettlebells, or trap implements

**5. Difficulty:** Beginner to intermediate

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Shrugging under the load
  - Leaning forward or side to side
  - Taking sloppy noisy steps
  - Using more load than grip can control cleanly

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Use a clear walking path and set the weights down safely once posture fades.
  - Choose a load you can carry without twisting or collapsing.

**8. Breathing:** Use steady rhythmic breaths without losing your brace or shrugging.

**9. Tempo:** Walk at a strong controlled pace with crisp repeatable steps.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Stand tall
  - Grip hard
  - Walk without wobble

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Suitcase carry with lighter load.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Stand tall".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add weight slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Increase load.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering part or add a pause to make it harder without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, add small amounts of weight or a rep or two week to week.

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Increase load
  - Increase distance
  - Use uneven surfaces only if control is solid

**15. Regressions:**
  - Suitcase carry with lighter load
  - Farmer hold
  - March in place under load

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Dead bug
  - Pallof press
  - Plank shoulder tap
  - Rower sprint

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Lower back: keep your back flat (not rounded) and don't lift more than you can control.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 20–40 meters or 30–45 sec per set

**19. Ideal rest period:** 60–90 sec

**20. Training purpose:** The farmer carry is a bilateral loaded carry where you walk with heavy weights at the sides. It trains posture, grip, and trunk stiffness while the legs keep moving under load.

**Setup:** Stand tall with one weight in each hand at the sides. Set the shoulders down, ribs stacked, and grip firm before you start walking.

**Execution:** Walk with short controlled steps while staying tall and resisting sway. Keep the weights quiet at the sides and finish the carry before posture starts to break down.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Stand tall with the weights at the sides, shoulders packed, and trunk braced before the first step.
  - Mid: Take short strong steps while keeping the torso vertical and the weights quiet.
  - Peak: Own the middle of the carry with full-body tension and no leaning or shrugging.
  - Finish: Complete the distance, slow the steps down, and set the weights down with control.
