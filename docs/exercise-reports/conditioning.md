## Agility ladder in-out

> 📝 Text guide (image pending) · Class: conditioning

**1. Exercise name:** Agility ladder in-out

**2. Primary muscles:** Conditioning, Calves, Hip flexors

**3. Secondary muscles:** Glutes, Adductors, Core

**4. Equipment:** Agility ladder

**5. Difficulty:** intermediate

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Looking straight down the whole time
  - Clipping the ladder because the pace is too high
  - Crossing the feet unintentionally
  - Letting the torso sway side to side

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Slow down before accuracy disappears.
  - Use a flat surface so the ladder does not bunch or slide.

**8. Breathing:** Use calm rhythmic breaths so the ladder pattern stays quick and crisp.

**9. Tempo:** Move quickly, but only as fast as you can place the feet cleanly in each square.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Eyes ahead
  - Fast accurate feet
  - Quiet hips

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Walk the pattern first.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Eyes ahead".
  - If you get tired, slow down or take a short break — don't push through sloppy or shaky form.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Shorter ladder pass.
  - Add difficulty by working longer, resting less, or going a bit faster while staying in control.

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body coordination — no special stretchiness needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Shorter ladder pass
  - Single-step in-out rhythm

**15. Regressions:**
  - Walk the pattern first
  - Shorter ladder pass

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Agility ladder lateral
  - Battle rope slams
  - Bear crawl cardio
  - Box step-up cardio

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Low joint stress. Stop if any sharp pain appears and make the movement smaller or easier.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 20–60 sec work intervals (or 30–90 sec steady)

**19. Ideal rest period:** 30–60 sec, or a 1:1 work:rest ratio

**20. Training purpose:** Agility ladder in-out is a footwork drill where the feet step in and out of each ladder square in a repeating rhythm that challenges coordination and conditioning.

**Setup:** Lay the ladder flat, stand at one end in an athletic stance, and look ahead enough to read the lane without staring straight down every step.

**Execution:** Step into the ladder square and back out in the planned pattern while the arms help rhythm and the torso stays centered over the feet.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the first stance just before the ladder with knees soft and eyes scanning a few rungs ahead.
  - Mid: Step into and out of the first squares with quick precise contacts and quiet hips.
  - Peak: Build to the fastest clean in-out rhythm you can hold without clipping the ladder.
  - Finish: Run out through the final rung, decelerate cleanly, and reset before the next pass.

**12. Advanced tips:**

  - Add difficulty by working longer, resting less, or going a bit faster while staying in control.

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body coordination — no special stretchiness needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Walk-through pace
  - Simple two-feet-per-square pattern

**15. Regressions:**
  - Shorter ladder segment
  - Walk-through pace

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Agility ladder in-out
  - Battle rope slams
  - Bear crawl cardio
  - Box step-up cardio

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Low joint stress. Stop if any sharp pain appears and make the movement smaller or easier.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 20–60 sec work intervals (or 30–90 sec steady)

## Assault bike sprint

> 📝 Text guide (image pending) · Class: conditioning

**1. Exercise name:** Assault bike sprint

**2. Primary muscles:** Conditioning, Quadriceps, Glutes, Arms

**3. Secondary muscles:** Calves, Core, Shoulders

**4. Equipment:** Air bike / assault bike

**5. Difficulty:** Intermediate to advanced

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Seat set too low or too high
  - Pulling with the arms but not driving the pedals
  - Bouncing around in the seat
  - Going all-out too early for the interval length

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Set the seat correctly before sprinting.
  - Use recovery periods that let you regain control before the next hard effort.

**8. Breathing:** Breathe hard and rhythmically rather than holding your breath during the sprint.

**9. Tempo:** Accelerate quickly into the work interval and sustain only as much pace as the interval demands.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Push and pull hard
  - Pedal with intent
  - Stay tall and rhythmic

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Bike sprint.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Push and pull hard".
  - If you get tired, slow down or take a short break — don't push through sloppy or shaky form.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Increase sprint duration.
  - Add difficulty by working longer, resting less, or going a bit faster while staying in control.

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body coordination — no special stretchiness needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Increase sprint duration
  - Increase repeat count
  - Use harder work-to-rest ratios

**15. Regressions:**
  - Bike sprint
  - Treadmill incline walk
  - Rower sprint

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Band power step
  - Battle rope waves
  - Bike sprint
  - Box jump

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Low joint stress. Stop if any sharp pain appears and make the movement smaller or easier.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 20–60 sec work intervals (or 30–90 sec steady)

**19. Ideal rest period:** 30–60 sec, or a 1:1 work:rest ratio

**20. Training purpose:** The assault bike sprint uses hard pedaling plus aggressive push-pull handle action to create a high-output conditioning interval with low impact.

**Setup:** Adjust the seat so you can pedal with a slight knee bend at the bottom. Set the hands on the moving handles, sit tall, and brace the trunk before building speed.

**Execution:** Drive the pedals hard while pushing and pulling the handles forcefully. Hold an efficient trunk position and keep the effort aggressive for the planned sprint interval.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set seat height, grip the handles, and begin pedaling with a stacked torso.
  - Mid: Drive into the sprint by pushing and pulling the handles while pedaling hard.
  - Peak: Hold the highest sustainable sprint output without bouncing in the saddle or losing rhythm.
  - Finish: Ease out of the sprint under control and keep moving for the recovery period instead of stopping abruptly.

## Band power step

**1. Exercise name:** Band power step

**2. Primary muscles:** Conditioning

**3. Secondary muscles:** Quads, Glutes, Core

**4. Equipment:** Resistance band

**5. Difficulty:** beginner

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Use a march if impact is not comfortable.
  - Shorter rounds work better than sloppy speed.

**8. Breathing:** Inhale on the controlled lowering phase and exhale as you drive through the main effort.

**9. Tempo:** Control the lowering phase, stay organized at the hardest point, and finish each rep before starting the next.

**10. Coaching cues:**

  - Fast arms help fast legs
  - Soft landings

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: High-knee march.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Tall posture".
  - If you get tired, slow down or take a short break — don't push through sloppy or shaky form.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Fast march in place.
  - Add difficulty by working longer, resting less, or going a bit faster while staying in control.

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body coordination — no special stretchiness needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Fast march in place
  - Low-impact cardio step

**15. Regressions:**
  - High-knee march
  - Fast march in place

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Assault bike sprint
  - Battle rope waves
  - Bike sprint
  - Box jump

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - This is a higher-stress movement — warm up well and add weight slowly.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 20–60 sec work intervals (or 30–90 sec steady)

**19. Ideal rest period:** 30–60 sec, or a 1:1 work:rest ratio

**20. Training purpose:** Band power step is a conditioning pattern matched to resistance band. used to train conditioning.

**Setup:** Set the band tension first, stand tall, and load the stance leg before you move.

**Execution:** Step explosively in the intended direction while the trunk stays braced and the band stays under control. Own the landing on the outside leg instead of letting the band whip you into position. Step back to the start under control and re-brace before the next rep.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the band tension first, stand tall, and load the stance leg before you move.
  - Mid: Step explosively in the intended direction while the trunk stays braced and the band stays under control.
  - Peak: Own the landing on the outside leg instead of letting the band whip you into position.
  - Finish: Step back to the start under control and re-brace before the next rep.

## Battle rope slams

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: conditioning

**1. Exercise name:** Battle rope slams

**2. Primary muscles:** Conditioning, Lats, Core

**3. Secondary muscles:** Shoulders, Glutes, Quadriceps

**4. Equipment:** Battle ropes

**5. Difficulty:** intermediate

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Arching the low back overhead
  - Slamming with straight locked knees
  - Letting the ropes drift with no clear finish
  - Using only the arms with no trunk control

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Keep enough room from the anchor so the ropes can move freely.
  - Back off if low-back fatigue replaces powerful trunk bracing.

**8. Breathing:** Exhale sharply on each slam and take quick recovery breaths between waves of effort.

**9. Tempo:** Attack each slam hard, then reset just enough to make the next rep crisp instead of muscling a slow rope drop.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Brace before the lift
  - Slam with the whole body
  - Reset each rep cleanly

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Alternating battle rope waves.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Brace before the lift".
  - If you get tired, slow down or take a short break — don't push through sloppy or shaky form.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Half-kneeling rope slams.
  - Add difficulty by working longer, resting less, or going a bit faster while staying in control.

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body coordination — no special stretchiness needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Half-kneeling rope slams
  - Shorter slam intervals

**15. Regressions:**
  - Alternating battle rope waves
  - Half-kneeling rope slams

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Agility ladder in-out
  - Agility ladder lateral
  - Bear crawl cardio
  - Box step-up cardio

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Low joint stress. Stop if any sharp pain appears and make the movement smaller or easier.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 20–60 sec work intervals (or 30–90 sec steady)

**19. Ideal rest period:** 30–60 sec, or a 1:1 work:rest ratio

**20. Training purpose:** Battle rope slams combine a fast overhead lift with a sharp downward strike, producing a powerful cardio interval that also trains full-body coordination.

**Setup:** Stand in an athletic stance holding one rope end in each hand, with enough distance from the anchor for visible rope movement.

**Execution:** Lift the hands overhead with the trunk braced, then slam the ropes down hard by driving the arms and torso together while keeping the knees soft.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the athletic stance, grip the rope ends firmly, and brace before the first overhead lift.
  - Mid: Raise the ropes overhead while the ribs stay stacked and the hips stay loaded.
  - Peak: Drive the ropes down explosively, using the trunk and arms together to create a sharp slam.
  - Finish: Recoil into the next rep under control and stop the set before posture collapses.

  - Longer work intervals

## Burpee

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: conditioning

**1. Exercise name:** Burpee

**2. Primary muscles:** Quads, Chest, Shoulders

**3. Secondary muscles:** Triceps, Core, Glutes, Calves

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Step the feet instead of jumping if impact or control is a limiter.
  - Keep the plank phase clean rather than chasing speed at all costs.
  - Stop if low-back, wrist, or shoulder discomfort builds quickly.

**8. Breathing:** Exhale during the explosive stand or jump and use quick recovery breaths between reps.

**9. Tempo:** Move with a controlled drop, crisp plank transition, and strong stand back up.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Hands plant cleanly

  - Stand back up with control

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Squat thrust.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Hands plant cleanly".
  - If you get tired, slow down or take a short break — don't push through sloppy or shaky form.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Add a jump.
  - Add difficulty by working longer, resting less, or going a bit faster while staying in control.

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body coordination — no special stretchiness needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Add a jump
  - Increase pace
  - Use interval sets with controlled rest

**15. Regressions:**
  - Squat thrust
  - Mountain climber
  - High plank walkout

**16. Alternative exercises:**

  - Band power step

  - Bike sprint

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Step onto the moving belt only once the speed is set low enough to establish a stable stride.
  - Mid: Settle into a natural walking rhythm with full-foot pressure and relaxed arm swing.
  - Peak: Own the middle of the interval with steady posture and repeatable cadence instead of drifting on the handles.
  - Finish: Reduce the speed gradually and step off only when the belt is safe and your balance is steady.
