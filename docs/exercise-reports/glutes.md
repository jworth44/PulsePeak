## Band glute kickback

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: isolation

**1. Exercise name:** Band glute kickback

**2. Primary muscles:** Glutes

**3. Secondary muscles:** Hamstrings, Core

**4. Equipment:** Resistance band

**5. Difficulty:** Beginner

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Letting the pelvis rotate open
  - Using body swing instead of glute tension
  - Arching the low back
  - Using an unstable band anchor

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Check the band and anchor before starting.
  - Reduce tension if you cannot keep the torso steady.

**8. Breathing:** Exhale as you kick back. Inhale as you return.

**9. Tempo:** Kick back smoothly and return for 2-3 seconds under tension.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Square pelvis
  - Glute drives the leg back
  - Band returns slowly

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Glute bridge.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Square pelvis".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add weight slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Use a stronger band.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering part or add a pause to make it harder without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, add small amounts of weight or a rep or two week to week.

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Use a stronger band
  - Pause at full extension
  - Slow the eccentric return

**15. Regressions:**
  - Glute bridge
  - Quadruped hip extension
  - Bodyweight kickback

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Cable glute kickback
  - Cable pull-through
  - Hip thrust

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Elbows/wrists: move smoothly and don't jerk or fully snap the joint straight.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 10–15 reps

**19. Ideal rest period:** 45–60 sec

**20. Training purpose:** The band glute kickback is a home-friendly hip-extension exercise performed against band tension. It is useful when you want direct glute work without a cable station.

**Setup:** Loop the band around the working ankle and anchor it securely behind you or around the support leg depending on the setup. Stand tall, hold a stable surface if needed, and brace the trunk.

**Execution:** Drive the working leg back by extending the hip while keeping the pelvis square. Squeeze the glute at the end of the rep and return forward slowly without letting the band snap you in.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the band securely, organize your balance, and begin with the working foot slightly under the hip.
  - Mid: Extend the hip and send the leg back while the torso stays quiet.
  - Peak: Hold the end position briefly with the glute squeezed and the pelvis still square.
  - Finish: Bring the leg back in slowly while keeping tension on the band.

## Banded glute bridge

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: general

**1. Exercise name:** Banded glute bridge

**2. Primary muscles:** Glutes

**3. Secondary muscles:** Hamstrings, Core

**4. Equipment:** Resistance band

**5. Difficulty:** beginner

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Driving from the low back
  - Feet too far from the hips
  - Rushing the lowering phase

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Keep range smaller if the back or front of the hip gets irritated.
  - Pause and reset if hamstrings cramp early.

**8. Breathing:** Inhale on the controlled lowering phase and exhale as you drive through the main effort.

**9. Tempo:** Control the lowering phase, stay organized at the hardest point, and finish each rep before starting the next.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Ribs stay down
  - Push through heels
  - Squeeze glutes at the top

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Bridge hold.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Ribs stay down".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add weight slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Single-leg bridge.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering part or add a pause to make it harder without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, add small amounts of weight or a rep or two week to week.

**13. Mobility requirements:** hamstring and hip flexibility to bend forward from the hips with a flat back.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Single-leg bridge
  - Banded bridge

**15. Regressions:**
  - Bridge hold
  - Single-leg bridge

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Barbell hip thrust pause
  - Cable pull-through
  - Dumbbell glute bridge
  - Frog pump

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Lower back: keep your back flat (not rounded) and don't lift more than you can control.
  - Elbows/wrists: move smoothly and don't jerk or fully snap the joint straight.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 8–12 reps

**19. Ideal rest period:** 60–90 sec

**20. Training purpose:** Banded glute bridge is a glutes pattern matched to resistance band. used to train glutes.

**Setup:** Plant the feet, set the ribs down, and position the load over the hips before you begin the first rep.

**Execution:** Drive through the heels to lift the hips, squeeze the glutes at the top, and lower under control without arching the low back.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Plant the feet, brace lightly, and set the ribs before you lift.
  - Mid: Push through the heels and lift the hips until the glutes do the work.
  - Peak: Pause at the top without turning the lockout into a low-back arch.
  - Finish: Lower slowly and reset so the hips do not just drop between reps.

## Cable glute kickback

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: isolation

**1. Exercise name:** Cable glute kickback

**2. Primary muscles:** Glutes

**3. Secondary muscles:** Hamstrings, Core

**4. Equipment:** Cable machine, Ankle cuff

**5. Difficulty:** Beginner

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Arching the low back to move the leg farther
  - Swinging the leg with momentum
  - Rotating the pelvis open
  - Letting the cable yank the leg back in

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Keep the motion driven from the hip, not the lower back.
  - Use a load you can control without twisting the pelvis.

**8. Breathing:** Exhale as you kick back. Inhale as you return.

**9. Tempo:** Kick back for 1-2 seconds and return for 2-3 seconds under control.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Hip extends, low back stays quiet
  - Squeeze the glute
  - Return under tension

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Band glute kickback.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Hip extends, low back stays quiet".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add weight slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Add a pause at full extension.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering part or add a pause to make it harder without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, add small amounts of weight or a rep or two week to week.

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Add a pause at full extension
  - Increase load gradually
  - Use slow eccentric returns

**15. Regressions:**
  - Band glute kickback
  - Glute bridge
  - Frog pump

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Band glute kickback
  - Cable pull-through
  - Hip thrust

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Elbows/wrists: move smoothly and don't jerk or fully snap the joint straight.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 10–15 reps

**19. Ideal rest period:** 45–60 sec

**20. Training purpose:** The cable glute kickback isolates hip extension by driving one leg back against cable resistance. It is useful for direct glute work when you want less spinal loading than hinges or squats.

**Setup:** Attach an ankle cuff to a low cable, face the machine, and hold onto the frame for balance. Stand tall on the support leg, brace lightly, and keep the working knee soft before kicking back.

**Execution:** Extend the hip by driving the leg back without arching the lower back. Squeeze the glute at the end range, then return the foot forward slowly until tension stays on the cable.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the cuff, hold the machine for balance, and begin with the working foot slightly under the hip.
  - Mid: Drive the leg back by extending the hip while the torso stays quiet and ribs controlled.
  - Peak: Reach the strongest glute contraction with the leg behind you and no low-back arch.
  - Finish: Return the foot forward slowly until the cable stays taut and you can repeat cleanly.

## Cable pull-through

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: general

**1. Exercise name:** Cable pull-through

**2. Primary muscles:** Glutes

**3. Secondary muscles:** Hamstrings, Core

**4. Equipment:** Machine or cable station

**5. Difficulty:** intermediate

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Overarching the back
  - Feet too far away
  - Rushing the top position

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Use bodyweight or glute bridge first if setup feels unstable.
  - Stop short of painful hip pinching.

**8. Breathing:** Inhale on the controlled lowering phase and exhale as you drive through the main effort.

**9. Tempo:** Control the lowering phase, stay organized at the hardest point, and finish each rep before starting the next.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Drive through heels
  - Ribs down at the top
  - Squeeze glutes, not low back

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Glute bridge.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Drive through heels".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add weight slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Dumbbell hip thrust.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering part or add a pause to make it harder without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, add small amounts of weight or a rep or two week to week.

**13. Mobility requirements:** the ability to pull with your back by squeezing your shoulder blades together.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Dumbbell hip thrust
  - Paused bodyweight bridge

**15. Regressions:**
  - Glute bridge
  - Dumbbell hip thrust

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Band glute kickback
  - Banded glute bridge
  - Barbell hip thrust pause
  - Cable glute kickback

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Elbows/wrists: move smoothly and don't jerk or fully snap the joint straight.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 8–12 reps

**19. Ideal rest period:** 60–90 sec

**20. Training purpose:** Cable pull-through is a hip hinge that trains the glutes and hamstrings with cable tension pulling from behind.

**Setup:** Stand facing away from the cable, hold the rope between the legs, and step far enough forward to keep tension at the start.

**Execution:** Hinge back into the hips, keep the rope close to the body, then drive the hips through to stand tall without leaning back.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Face away from the cable, step forward to create tension, and hinge back with the rope close to the hips.
  - Mid: Let the hips travel back until the glutes and hamstrings are loaded and the spine stays long.
  - Peak: Push the floor away and bring the hips through until you stand tall with the glutes squeezed.
  - Finish: Guide the rope back between the legs under control and rebuild the hinge before the next rep.

## Clamshell

> 📝 Text guide (image pending) · Class: isolation

**1. Exercise name:** Clamshell

**2. Primary muscles:** Glute medius

**3. Secondary muscles:** Glute minimus, Deep hip rotators

**4. Equipment:** Bodyweight or mini band

**5. Difficulty:** Beginner

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Rolling the pelvis backward to create more range
  - Letting the feet separate
  - Moving too fast to feel the side hip
  - Using a range that shifts the work into the low back

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Keep the movement small enough that the lateral hip stays loaded.
  - Add a pillow or pad under the head and knees if the side-lying setup is uncomfortable.

**8. Breathing:** Exhale as the top knee opens. Inhale as it lowers.

**9. Tempo:** Open for 1-2 seconds, pause briefly, and lower for 2-3 seconds.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Feet stay together
  - Top knee opens from the hip
  - Pelvis stays stacked

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Side-lying isometric hold.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Feet stay together".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add weight slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Add a mini band.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering part or add a pause to make it harder without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, add small amounts of weight or a rep or two week to week.

**13. Mobility requirements:** the ability to turn at the upper body while keeping your lower back still.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Add a mini band
  - Pause at the top
  - Progress to lateral band walks

**15. Regressions:**
  - Side-lying isometric hold
  - Hip abduction with very small range
  - Supine band abduction

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Hip abduction
  - Lateral band walk

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Low joint stress. Stop if any sharp pain appears and make the movement smaller or easier.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 10–15 reps

**19. Ideal rest period:** 45–60 sec

**20. Training purpose:** The clamshell is a side-lying glute exercise where the top knee opens while the feet stay together. It targets the lateral hip and deep rotators without needing much external load.

**Setup:** Lie on one side with hips and knees bent, feet stacked, and head supported comfortably. Keep the pelvis stacked and the heels together before starting the rep.

**Execution:** Open the top knee while keeping the feet touching and the pelvis from rolling backward. Raise only as high as you can keep the side hip doing the work, then lower with control.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the side-lying position with knees bent, feet together, and hips stacked.
  - Mid: Lift the top knee by rotating from the hip while the feet stay connected.
  - Peak: Hold the highest position you can control without the pelvis rolling back.
  - Finish: Lower the knee slowly until it meets the bottom leg and repeat.

## Frog pump

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: isolation

**1. Exercise name:** Frog pump

**2. Primary muscles:** Glutes

**3. Secondary muscles:** Hamstrings, Deep hip rotators

**4. Equipment:** Bodyweight or dumbbell

**5. Difficulty:** Beginner

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Pushing mostly through the low back instead of the glutes
  - Letting the feet drift too far away
  - Dropping the hips too quickly between reps
  - Losing the knee-open position

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Use a smaller top range if the front of the hips feels pinchy.
  - Keep the ribs controlled so the low back does not over-arch.

**8. Breathing:** Exhale as you lift the hips. Inhale as you lower.

**9. Tempo:** Lift for 1-2 seconds, squeeze briefly, and lower for 2 seconds.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Soles together, knees open
  - Glutes drive the lift
  - Lower softly

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Glute bridge.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Soles together, knees open".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add weight slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Add a dumbbell to the hips.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering part or add a pause to make it harder without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, add small amounts of weight or a rep or two week to week.

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Add a dumbbell to the hips
  - Use higher reps with pauses
  - Progress to hip thrusts

**15. Regressions:**
  - Glute bridge
  - Reduced-range bridge
  - Glute squeeze isometric

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Banded glute bridge
  - Barbell hip thrust pause
  - Cable pull-through
  - Dumbbell glute bridge

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Elbows/wrists: move smoothly and don't jerk or fully snap the joint straight.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 10–15 reps

**19. Ideal rest period:** 45–60 sec

**20. Training purpose:** The frog pump is a glute bridge variation performed with the soles of the feet together and the knees open. The shortened leg position reduces hamstring contribution and helps many people feel the glutes more clearly.

**Setup:** Lie on your back, bring the soles of the feet together, and let the knees fall outward. Keep the heels close enough to the hips to create leverage and brace lightly through the trunk.

**Execution:** Drive the hips upward by squeezing the glutes and pressing through the outer edges of the feet. Pause briefly at the top, then lower until the hips touch down softly before the next rep.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set on your back with soles together, knees open, and heels close enough to the hips for a strong bridge.
  - Mid: Drive the hips upward while keeping the knees open and the low back from taking over.
  - Peak: Squeeze the glutes hard at the top with the hips fully extended and ribs controlled.
  - Finish: Lower the hips back down softly and reset the feet before the next rep.

  - Reduced-range bridge

**20. Training purpose:** Hip abduction moves the thigh outward away from the midline to train the glute medius and minimus. It is useful for building lateral hip strength that supports squats, lunges, and gait mechanics.

**Setup:** Set up in the machine or band position with the pelvis stable and the working line of force aligned with the thighs. Sit or stand tall and keep the torso from swaying as the leg moves outward.

**Execution:** Move the thighs or leg outward under control until the lateral hip is fully engaged. Pause briefly, then return slowly without letting the resistance pull you back too fast.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the pelvis steady in the machine or band position and begin with the legs in the start range.
  - Mid: Press the thigh or knees outward while the torso stays quiet and the feet stay organized.
  - Peak: Reach the strongest lateral-hip contraction without rolling the pelvis or trunk.
  - Finish: Return inward slowly until the start position is reached with control.

<a id="hip-thrust"></a>
## Hip thrust

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: compound

**1. Exercise name:** Hip thrust

**2. Primary muscles:** Glutes

**3. Secondary muscles:** Hamstrings, Core, Adductors

**4. Equipment:** Barbell or dumbbell, Bench

**5. Difficulty:** Intermediate

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Overarching the low back instead of finishing with the glutes
  - Setting the feet too far away or too close
  - Driving mostly through the toes
  - Losing upper-back support on the bench
  - Dropping the load too quickly

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Pad the hips if the load position is uncomfortable.
  - Use a setup that keeps the neck relaxed and the upper back securely supported.
  - Reduce load if the movement shifts into the low back instead of the glutes.

**8. Breathing:** Inhale before driving up. Exhale near the top as the hips lock out under control.

**9. Tempo:** Lift with steady power, pause briefly at the top, and lower for 2-3 seconds.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Drive through the heels
  - Ribs stay down
  - Finish with glutes not low back

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Glute bridge.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Drive through the heels".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add weight slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Increase load gradually.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering part or add a pause to make it harder without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, add small amounts of weight or a rep or two week to week.

**13. Mobility requirements:** hamstring and hip flexibility to bend forward from the hips with a flat back.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Increase load gradually
  - Pause longer at the top
  - Use single-leg variations later

**15. Regressions:**
  - Glute bridge
  - Bodyweight hip thrust
  - Frog pump

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Band glute kickback
  - Banded glute bridge
  - Barbell hip thrust pause
  - Cable glute kickback

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Lower back: keep your back flat (not rounded) and don't lift more than you can control.
  - Elbows/wrists: move smoothly and don't jerk or fully snap the joint straight.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 6–10 reps (strength: 4–6 · muscle: 8–12)

**19. Ideal rest period:** 90–180 sec (heavier = longer)

**20. Training purpose:** The hip thrust is a loaded hip-extension exercise performed with the upper back supported on a bench. It emphasizes the glutes and allows heavy loading without the same spinal demands as some hinge patterns.

**Setup:** Set the upper back on a bench, position the load over the hips, and plant the feet so the shins can be close to vertical near the top. Brace the trunk and keep the chin slightly tucked.

**Execution:** Drive through the heels and extend the hips until the torso and thighs form a strong line. Keep the ribs controlled and avoid arching through the low back. Lower under control until the hips return to the start.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the upper back on the bench, place the load over the hips, plant the feet, and brace before lifting.
  - Mid: Drive through the heels and extend the hips while keeping the ribs controlled and the chin tucked.
  - Peak: Reach full hip extension with the glutes squeezed and the torso level, not overarched.
  - Finish: Lower the hips under control until you return to the starting position and reset tension.

## Lateral band walk

> 📷 Full visual sequence · Class: general

**1. Exercise name:** Lateral band walk

**2. Primary muscles:** Glute medius

**3. Secondary muscles:** Glute minimus, Tensor fasciae latae, Core

**4. Equipment:** Resistance band

**5. Difficulty:** Beginner

**6. Common mistakes:**
  - Standing too tall and losing side-hip tension
  - Letting the knees cave inward during the trail step
  - Dragging the feet instead of stepping cleanly
  - Taking steps that are too big to control

**7. Safety warnings:**
  - Use a light enough band that you can keep the knees and feet aligned.
  - Keep the steps controlled to avoid twisting the knees.

**8. Breathing:** Breathe steadily throughout the set and avoid holding your breath while staying braced.

**9. Tempo:** Take controlled side steps and keep constant band tension rather than moving quickly.

**10. Coaching cues:**
  - Band stays taut
  - Knees track out
  - Trail foot follows under control

**11. Beginner tips:**
  - If it feels hard, start with an easier version: Hip abduction.
  - Focus on one thing first: "Band stays taut".
  - Use light or no weight until the movement feels smooth and repeatable, then add weight slowly.
  - Move slowly and watch yourself in a mirror or video to check your positions.

**12. Advanced tips:**
  - Make it harder: Place the band around the ankles.
  - Add tempo — slow the lowering part or add a pause to make it harder without more weight.
  - Once form is automatic, add small amounts of weight or a rep or two week to week.

**13. Mobility requirements:** general full-body control — no special mobility needed to start.

**14. Progressions:**
  - Place the band around the ankles
  - Use a lower athletic stance
  - Add longer walking sets

**15. Regressions:**
  - Hip abduction
  - Clamshell
  - Bodyweight side step

**16. Alternative exercises:**
  - Clamshell
  - Hip abduction

**17. Injury considerations:**
  - Low joint stress. Stop if any sharp pain appears and make the movement smaller or easier.

**18. Ideal rep range:** 8–12 reps

**19. Ideal rest period:** 60–90 sec

**20. Training purpose:** The lateral band walk is a side-stepping drill performed against band tension. It trains the lateral hips to resist the knees collapsing inward and supports stronger squat and lunge mechanics.

**Setup:** Place a loop band above the knees or around the ankles depending on the challenge you want. Set a shallow athletic stance with hips back slightly, feet parallel, and tension already on the band.

**Execution:** Step sideways under control without letting the band snap the trailing leg in. Keep the toes mostly forward, knees soft, and tension on the band as you continue stepping both directions.

**Visual sequence (steps):**
  - Start: Set the band position, sink into a slight squat, and create gentle tension before stepping.
  - Mid: Step sideways with the lead foot while the pelvis stays level and the knees stay tracking out.
  - Peak: Set the new stance width with the side hip engaged and the band still under tension.
  - Finish: Bring the trailing foot in carefully without losing the tension or letting the knees collapse.
